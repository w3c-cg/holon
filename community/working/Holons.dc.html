Systems Thinking

Holons

An Introduction

Definition
Whole complete in itself
Part member of a larger whole

A holon is any entity that is simultaneously a whole in its own right and a part of a larger system — neither purely reducible, nor purely independent.

"I have coined the word holon for these Janus-faced entities which are simultaneously wholes and parts."
Arthur Koestler The Ghost in the Machine, 1967
The Janus Face

Every holon has two tendencies

Self-assertive

Acts as an autonomous whole

Maintains its own identity, structure, and rules. Resists being dissolved into the larger system. Preserves its boundaries.

Integrative

Acts as a dependent part

Cooperates and coordinates with other holons. Subordinates itself to the needs of the larger system. Enables collective function.

Key Ideas

What to take away

1

Wholeness and partness are always relative, never absolute. Every entity exists at a scale.

2

New properties emerge at each level of a holarchy — properties that cannot be found in the parts below.

3

Healthy systems balance autonomy and integration — the two tendencies must coexist in every holon.

4

This pattern appears across every domain — nature, organizations, and technology all follow holonic structure.

Founding Argument

Why holons, why now

Flat graphs aren't enough

Existing knowledge graph architectures are powerful but flat — they lack native support for scope, temporality, and nested narrative context. Complex stateful domains need something more structured.

The AI integration moment

LLMs need grounded, scoped context to reason reliably. The convergence of RDF 1.2, SHACL, and MCP now provides the tooling to supply it. Demand for structured, bounded context has never been higher.

Community over vendor

This is not a moment for a single implementation. Shared conventions — for messaging, architecture, and interoperability — require a community effort rather than a proprietary standard.

Scope

Entities & scope

A containing holon may declare entities that its contained holons can reference — the graph equivalent of scope.

Agents are entities — items that change state and can be declared and shared across holons.

Including a holon grants access to its declared entities, with restrictions on what can be referenced.

A contained holon sees only what its containing holon has declared — and only what it has been granted permission to reference.

Semantic Graphs

One entity, many contexts

An employee declared in a company holon can be referenced by both an HR holon and an R&D holon — for entirely different purposes.

This is why holons and semantic graphs work so well together — both treat entities as meaningful across multiple contexts simultaneously.

Four Aspects

A holon's four aspects

Every holon consists of four distinct design partitions, each serving a different structural purpose.

These aren't necessarily graphs in the formal sense — they are conceptual partitions that define how a holon is structured and observed.

1

Scene Graph

Represents the internal state — components, relationships, and current configuration of the holon.

2

Event Graph

Records mutations to the agents — the history of how the system has changed over time.

3

Boundary

Rules and restrictions governing the system's evolution — what may change and how.

4

Projection Layers

Multiple views of the holon, each representing a different observer's perspective on the same system.

Aspect 1

The Scene Graph

The Scene is the knowledge graph for the holon — the stage on which the narrative is enacted.

It contains all participating entities and may also record their originating states — the baseline from which changes are measured.

Aspect 2

The Event Graph

Event graphs represent changes — what agents say or do, mutations of state, invalidations, and clock events.

In a stage play, this is the action log of the scene over time. Also known as the context graph.

act

An agent speaks, moves, or performs an action within the scene.

mutate

An agent's state is updated — properties, attributes, or relationships change.

invalidate

A resource or state is retired — no longer available for use.

clock

System time advances — triggering scheduled or time-dependent events.

Aspect 3

The Boundary

The Boundary represents constraints and performs mutations — governing what may change and how.

In RDF 1.2, this is implemented via SHACL shapes and rules — a formal language for expressing permitted and constrained changes.

Boundary

Character can walk through a door

Key is required to unlock the door (conditional)

Character cannot walk through a wall

A resource, once used, may not be usable again

RDF 1.2 — SHACL shapes and rules
Aspect 4

The Projection Layer

How the holon is seen from outside — mediated by cameras within the scene.

A camera is a designated observation point within the scene — a defined perspective or query that selects and filters what is visible from a given vantage. There will usually be multiple projections for any holon, each mediated by a different camera — each giving a different view of the same system.

Cameras bear the same relationship to projections that portals bear to holons — both are mediation boundaries that carry the effects of evolution without exposing internal state directly.

Audience Front-facing scene view
Aerial Map Spatial overview from above
Agent POV Scene from a specific agent

A camera is any means of performing observations within the holon.

State

Open and closed holons

A holon is either open — accepting events, validating inputs, presenting dynamic views — or closed, representing a complete or interrupted narrative.

Analogous to an in-memory object versus one that has been persisted.

Open
Accepts events
Validates inputs
Presents dynamic views
→ in-memory object
Closed

→ persisted object
Evolution

How open holons evolve

Open holons use several mechanisms to determine how they change over time.

Once closed, a holon becomes a record of that narrative — the script of a play, the transcript of a meeting.

Bayesian Priors

Actions informed by previous behaviors — the holon uses history to anticipate what to do next.

Active Inference

Measures surprise — the degree by which the expected state differs from the active state — driving toward lower uncertainty.

State Machines

Rules-based transitions define what states are reachable and under what conditions they may be entered.

External Actors

Agents controlled from outside the holon — humans or other systems that directly direct its evolution.

Serialization

Databooks

Markdown as a substrate — with a publishing-oriented metadata header and zero or more code blocks.

Carries all components of an in-flight holon, or stores data holons — taxonomy, schema, queries, and application metadata.

The messaging substrate of a holon architecture — equivalent to an HTML document, a play script, or a musical score.

A triple store or object database can be abstracted as a deep databook, or as a store of multiple databooks.

New Directions

Holons & AI

Scope, grounding, and intelligent authorship

Hallucinations

Grounding LLMs with holons

A major cause of hallucinations is a lack of scope — too much ambiguity in entity resolution.

Databooks provide that scope — making hallucinations far less likely, especially for chat interactions. They will not completely eliminate them, but they significantly reduce the ambiguity that causes them.

Databook scope provides
Entities currently in play Scene Graph
History of each entity Event Graph
Rules for state transitions Boundary
How information is projected to the user Projection Layer
Each maps directly to a holon aspect.
Authoring & Retrieval

AI as author and bridge

AI reduces the cost of composing holons — and holon architecture enables better AI output.

AI Composition

AI systems can compose data-centered and holon databooks using a skills.md architecture alongside MCP — minimising authoring cost and complexity considerably.

Holon Bridges

Provide mechanisms for retrieving and updating data stores — especially coupled with agentic systems and chat. Dialog-based AI benefits from the cues databooks provide when generating final output.

Process

Considerations

Interoperability

Focus is on interoperability. While early implementations will likely use the RDF 1.2 stack, the architecture itself should be data-format agnostic.

W3C Status

HCG specifications are not a W3C Endorsed Recommendation, though parts may be presented to the W3C for consideration under their processes at a later date.

IP Rights

IP rights policies are awaiting CG consensus, though likely will align with W3C policies with regard to licensing.
